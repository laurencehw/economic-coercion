# Comprehensive Editorial Review: *Economic Coercion in the 21st Century*

**Reviewer:** Claude (AI-assisted editorial review)
**Date:** February 23, 2026
**Scope:** All 10 chapters, front matter, bibliography, glossary, 10 tabletop exercises, appendix, ~60 data visualizations, and R code infrastructure

---

## Overall Score: 7.0 / 10

**Summary Verdict:** This is an ambitious, substantively rich, and analytically sophisticated graduate textbook that fills a genuine gap in the economic statecraft literature. The breadth of coverage -- from Napoleon's Continental System to AI export controls, from SWIFT exclusions to submarine cable vulnerabilities -- is impressive, and the consistent integration of Chinese perspectives distinguishes it from competitors. The analytical framework (four dimensions, five effectiveness criteria) is well-designed and pedagogically useful. However, the manuscript suffers from systematic issues that prevent it from reaching the 8-9 range: excessive length and redundancy across chapters, inconsistent citation practices, several factual errors requiring correction, data integrity concerns in the visualizations, and structural unevenness. With disciplined editing -- primarily cutting 20-25% of total wordcount and fixing citation/factual issues -- this could be an excellent textbook.

## Part I: Chapter-by-Chapter Assessment

### Chapter 1: Foundations of Economic Statecraft (7.5/10)

**Strengths:** The four-dimensional analytical framework (domain, target, objective, intensity) is the book's intellectual backbone and is clearly articulated. The CoCom and SWIFT/Russia case studies are well-executed. The Government Tools Boxes on IEEPA and the National Emergencies Act are unusually thorough. The opening vignette on the October 2022 semiconductor controls is vivid and effective.

**Weaknesses:** At ~20,000+ words, it is far too long. Key ideas repeat across sections (the October 2022 controls appear at least five times). The NEA Government Tools Box largely duplicates the IEEPA box. The engagement narrative (1979-2008) is somewhat hagiographic, overlooking pre-2008 skeptics like Aaron Friedberg. Missing discussion of domestic political economy -- both the US and China are treated as unitary actors.

**Key Factual Issues:**
- The FDPR was not "novel" in 2022 -- it was applied to Huawei in 2020; the 2022 *expansion* was novel
- Iran oil exports of "~200,000 barrels/day at peak effectiveness" is at the low end of estimates; most analysts estimated 300,000-500,000 bpd
- "2.4 million U.S. jobs" lost (Autor, Dorn, Hanson) is a contested estimate that should be noted as such
- Boeing "25% of revenue from Chinese customers" needs a specific time period

**Recommendations:** Cut 25-30%. Add a visual diagram of the four-dimensional framework. Consolidate the two Government Tools Boxes. Add domestic political economy discussion.

### Chapter 2: Supply Chain Vulnerabilities and Dependencies (7.5/10)

**Strengths:** The four-condition chokepoint test (concentration, substitution difficulty, criticality, time to build alternatives) is analytically precise and immediately applicable. The semiconductor supply chain walkthrough is outstanding. The Chinese Perspective Box is superb -- presenting internal Chinese debates about the costs of self-reliance. Honest assessment of mitigation strategies.

**Weaknesses:** Also excessively long (~18,000+ words). The COVID-19 PPE narrative is too granular. No formal case study analyzed through the Chapter 1 framework (a structural gap). Over-reliance on the TSMC "90% of advanced chips" figure (repeated six times without defining "advanced"). India is not adequately distinguished from China in the pharmaceutical supply chain discussion. The pharmaceutical contamination scenario is speculative and somewhat alarmist.

**Key Factual Issues:**
- TSMC's "90%" depends heavily on how "advanced" is defined -- needs a node threshold
- "China controls approximately 60% of lithium processing" varies by source and year
- Fab costs of "$15-20 billion" may already be dated (recent projects: $20-40 billion)
- "20-30 million gallons daily" water usage for a single fab needs sourcing

**Recommendations:** Add a formal case study (the 2010 rare earth dispute is an obvious candidate). Differentiate India from China in the pharma discussion. Add a summary table of key chokepoints.

### Chapter 3: Critical Sectors and Strategic Resources (7.0/10)

**Strengths:** Compelling opening with the February 2022 Russian invasion and Europe's gas vulnerability. Systematic coverage of food, energy, water, and minerals. The fertilizer dependency discussion is a particular strength. The "Green Transition Paradox" is memorably framed.

**Weaknesses:** Significant redundancy -- the rare earth discussion explicitly acknowledges overlap with Chapters 2 and 9 yet still retells the 2010 incident. The Green Transition Paradox is stated at least four times. The water section is too thin relative to its claimed importance. Many institutional citations (IEA 2022, FAO 2022) lack corresponding entries in the References section.

**Key Factual Issues:**
- The GERD was not "completed in 2020" -- it began filling in 2020 but was not complete
- The U.S. grain embargo was January 1980, not "the 1970s"
- "British households saw energy bills triple" is imprecise; the price cap roughly doubled
- Saudi spare capacity is typically 1-2 million bpd, not "millions" (plural)
- Morocco's phosphate reserves include disputed Western Sahara deposits (should be noted)

**Recommendations:** Cut 15-20% through eliminating redundancy. Correct factual errors. Expand the water section or justify its brevity. Reconcile in-text citations with the reference list.

### Chapter 4: Technology and Innovation Competition (7.5/10)

**Strengths:** Outstanding opening on the October 2022 controls. The semiconductor analysis is the chapter's strongest component -- thorough on EUV/DUV lithography, allied coordination tensions, and the Huawei Mate 60 Pro. The five-criteria effectiveness assessment is analytically rigorous. The Chinese Perspective Box addresses Big Fund corruption -- an issue many textbooks sanitize.

**Weaknesses:** At ~680 lines, excessively long. Semiconductor export controls are analyzed in detail twice (main section and case study, with ~60-70% overlap). The AI section is less original than the semiconductor section. The emerging technologies section (quantum, space, biotech) is uneven -- space and biotech are too brief to add value. Limited engagement with counterarguments on export control effectiveness. Citation mismatches between text and references.

**Key Factual Issues:**
- EDA market: Siemens EDA (Mentor Graphics) is German-owned, complicating "three American companies"
- "100% of EDA software" overstates -- should be "dominant market share for leading-edge tools"
- EUV machine cost of "$150-200 million" applies to standard EUV; High-NA EUV costs $350M+
- China's orbital launches are not "comparable to or exceeding U.S. totals" -- the U.S. launched roughly 2x more in 2023

**Recommendations:** Merge the main semiconductor section and case study to eliminate redundancy. Engage with counterarguments (e.g., Jon Bateman's work on limits of technology denial). Either expand or cut the space/biotech subsections.

### Chapter 5: Information, Standards, and Digital Infrastructure (7.5/10)

**Strengths:** Strong conceptual framing of what distinguishes the information domain. Thorough treatment of cyber espionage economics. Nuanced Volt Typhoon analysis comparing cyber deterrence to nuclear deterrence. Excellent data localization comparison across China, EU, Russia, and India. Discussion questions are among the best in the textbook.

**Weaknesses:** Figure 5.1 is sourced from `fig_03_05_subsea_cables.png` (Chapter 3 figure -- numbering error). The executive summary at ~17 paragraphs is too long. The information warfare and influence operations section is comparatively thin. Near-complete absence of Russian cyber operations (SolarWinds). The TikTok narrative may date quickly. The Splinternet section lacks analytical bite. No formal model for how information-domain coercion differs from other types.

**Key Factual Issues:**
- "Over 95% of intercontinental data travels via undersea cables" -- should cite TeleGeography
- Huawei's 5G patent share of "15-20%" varies significantly by source
- Huawei revenue figures are rounded aggressively ($140B to $100B should be more precise)
- Chinese VC investment decline figures need explicit Rhodium Group citation

**Recommendations:** Fix figure numbering. Trim the executive summary. Add brief treatment of Russian cyber operations. Add a discussion question on U.S. cyber norm compliance. Develop a formal analytical framework for information-domain coercion.

### Chapter 6: Trade Controls and Tariffs (7.0/10)

**Strengths:** Exceptional treatment of legal authorities (Section 232, 301, 201, EAR). The trade war timeline is meticulous. Empirical rigor on tariff incidence is a model of integrating academic research. The CoCom case study provides excellent historical depth. The European Perspective Box on experiencing coercion "from both directions" is analytically sharp.

**Weaknesses:** Substantial overlap with Chapter 4 on export controls. Government Tools Boxes are partially redundant with the main text. Internal inconsistency on Phase One purchase shortfall ($140 billion vs. $288 billion). At ~1,800 lines, the chapter is too long. No treatment of services trade restrictions. Missing discussion of the 2025 tariff escalation despite other content being current through 2026.

**Key Factual Issues:**
- Phase One shortfall: $140 billion and $288 billion cannot both be correct -- reconcile
- WTO DS543 panel ruling described as "(2024, expected)" -- it was circulated September 15, 2020
- "Three American companies" for EDA is inaccurate -- Siemens EDA is German-owned
- "Flaaen and Pierce (2024) estimate 30-40% of trade flow changes are irreversible" -- this specific paper needs verification

**Recommendations:** Resolve the $140B vs. $288B inconsistency. Correct the WTO ruling date. Reduce redundancy with Chapter 4 and between main text and Tools Boxes. Add a section on services trade. Update the trade war timeline through early 2025.

### Chapter 7: Financial Statecraft and Sanctions (7.5/10)

**Strengths:** Excellent organizational arc (structural foundations → institutional machinery → extraterritorial reach → erosion dynamics). The self-undermining paradox as organizing theme gives genuine analytical spine. The Global South Perspective Box is exceptional. Balanced treatment of de-dollarization. The QWERTY analogy for network effects is effective.

**Weaknesses:** At 1,254 lines, the chapter is extremely long. The Chinese Perspective Box substantially reiterates content already in the de-dollarization section. Russia's frozen reserves are mentioned at least six times with nearly identical framing. Missing dedicated Iran case study (JCPOA pressure campaign). Russia sanctions effectiveness is underdeveloped (binary "did it work?" frame). No substantive treatment of sanctions evasion networks.

**Key Factual Issues:**
- "Four days after Russia's full-scale invasion" -- the invasion was Feb 24; sanctions announcement was Feb 26 (two days, not four)
- BIS 2022 source cited for 2024 data
- Zarate 2013 cited for 2016-2017 events (book predates the events)
- Nephew 2017 cited for 2017-2019 export data (anachronistic)
- CIPS volume discrepancy: $24.5 trillion annually vs. implied ~$12.5 trillion from daily averages

**Recommendations:** Consolidate CIPS/digital yuan content. Add a dedicated Iran/JCPOA case study. Develop multi-dimensional Russia sanctions assessment. Address sanctions evasion more substantively. Fix citation anachronisms.

## Part II: Cross-Cutting Issues

### A. Length and Redundancy (Most Critical Issue)

The manuscript is significantly too long. Conservative estimates place the total at 150,000-180,000 words across 10 chapters -- equivalent to two standard academic books. Every chapter suffers from some combination of:

- **Internal redundancy:** Key statistics repeated multiple times within the same chapter (TSMC's 90%, Russia's $300B frozen assets)
- **Inter-chapter redundancy:** The October 2022 semiconductor controls are described in detail in Chapters 1, 2, 4, 6, and 10. The 2010 rare earth dispute appears in Chapters 2, 3, 6, and 9. The Huawei Mate 60 Pro appears in Chapters 1, 2, 4, 6, and 8.
- **Redundancy between main text and special boxes:** Government Tools Boxes often repeat content from the surrounding text

**Recommendation:** Target a 20-25% overall reduction. Establish a "home chapter" for each major case/event and use brief cross-references elsewhere. Consolidate Government Tools Boxes with main text.

### B. Citation and Reference Hygiene

This is a systematic problem across the entire manuscript:

- **Anachronistic citations:** Sources cited for events that postdate their publication (Zarate 2013 for 2016-2017 events; Nephew 2017 for 2017-2019 data)
- **Missing reference entries:** Many in-text institutional citations (IEA 2022, FAO 2022, World Bank 2022) lack corresponding entries in chapter reference lists
- **Inconsistent formats:** Mix of parenthetical author-date, narrative citations, and bare institutional names
- **Vague citations:** "Research suggests" and "studies show" without specific attribution
- **Mismatched text-bibliography:** Some references listed are never cited in text; some in-text citations are absent from references

**Recommendation:** Conduct a systematic citation audit across all chapters. Standardize to author-date format throughout. Ensure every factual claim has a specific, verifiable citation.

### C. Factual Accuracy

Across all 10 chapters, the review identified approximately 40-50 specific factual claims that need verification or correction. The most consequential include:

1. **WTO DS543 ruling date** (Ch. 6): Described as "(2024, expected)" -- actually circulated September 2020
2. **Phase One purchase shortfall** (Ch. 6): Internal inconsistency ($140B vs. $288B)
3. **GERD completion date** (Ch. 3): Not completed in 2020
4. **Grain embargo date** (Ch. 3): January 1980, not "the 1970s"
5. **Russia sanctions timeline** (Ch. 7): Two days after invasion, not four
6. **Rand exchange rate** (Ch. 9): "Nearly half" should be "roughly 70%"
7. **EDA market ownership** (Chs. 4, 6): Siemens EDA is German-owned
8. **Several data points** appear to use 2022 data presented as 2024 (lithium production, Treasury holdings, China orbital launches)

**Recommendation:** Fact-check all quantitative claims against primary sources. Update data to the most recent available. Flag contested or estimated figures as such.

### D. Missing Figures

A significant disconnect exists between the figure production pipeline and the chapter text. Generated figures that are not referenced in any chapter include:

- `fig_09_02_apartheid_timeline.png` (not referenced in Ch. 9)
- `fig_09_04_sanctions_decision_tree.png` (not referenced in Ch. 9)
- `fig_09_05_historical_outcomes.png` (not referenced in Ch. 9)
- `fig_10_02_critical_minerals.png` (no evidence of generation but script exists)
- `fig_10_04_decoupling_scenarios.png` (not referenced in Ch. 10)
- `fig_10_05_new_dependencies.png` (not referenced in Ch. 10)
- `fig_06_07_equipment_market_share.R` (script exists, no reference found in Ch. 6)
- `fig_07_07_dedollarization.R` (script exists, uncertain reference in Ch. 7)
- `fig_08_06_industrial_policy_compare.R` / `fig_08_07_bri_debt.R` / `fig_08_08_cfius_chinese_share.R` (scripts exist)

Additionally, Chapter 5's Figure 5.1 is sourced from a Chapter 3 figure file (`fig_03_05_subsea_cables.png`).

**Recommendation:** Audit every figure reference in every chapter. Integrate the missing figures. Fix figure numbering discontinuities.

### E. Data Visualization Concerns

The visualization program is solidly above average but has systemic weaknesses:

- **Data provenance:** No codebooks, no metadata files, no documentation of when data was downloaded or what transformations were applied. Some time series appear suspiciously smooth (SWIFT currency data, AI publications) -- monotonic trends year-over-year that do not match known real-world fluctuations.
- **Accessibility:** Color palettes have not been tested for colorblind safety. Several color pairings (India/Africa, Canada/EU) are problematic under deuteranopia.
- **Consistency:** Map figures (using `tmap`) have different visual styling from chart figures (using `ggplot2 + theme_econ_textbook()`).
- **Reproducibility:** No `renv.lock` or package management. Deprecated ggplot2 syntax in several scripts.
- **Specific issues:** Figure 7.1 includes a pie chart (poor practice). Figure 8.1's BRI corridor lines cross landmasses inappropriately. Figure 1.1's connecting arrows are too short to be visible.

**Recommendation:** Create data codebooks for all CSV files. Audit data accuracy against primary sources. Run colorblind simulations. Replace the pie chart. Add `renv` for package management.

## Part III: Tabletop Exercises Assessment (7.5/10)

**Strengths:** The exercises demonstrate strong pedagogical design with active learning integration, role differentiation creating productive tension, well-calibrated escalation via injects, and genuinely excellent debrief questions. The consistent four-phase structure (scenario → escalation → decision → debrief) is professional and usable. Exercises are well-grounded in real-world data and legal authorities.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Overwhelmingly U.S.-centric:** All ten exercises place participants exclusively in U.S. government roles. Not a single exercise asks students to play Chinese, European, or developing-country decision-makers as the primary team.
- **No defensive/proactive exercises:** All scenarios are reactive crisis management. None asks students to build economic resilience before a crisis.
- **Chapter 9 exercise is the weakest:** More seminar discussion than tabletop exercise; lacks the role tension and escalation that make others effective.
- **Chapter 10 exercise is overloaded:** Five simultaneous crisis domains in 90 minutes risks superficiality.
- **Content duplication:** The appendix duplicates the standalone exercise files, creating maintenance risk.
- **Temporal dating will age poorly:** Exercises set on specific near-future dates (some already past).

**Recommendations:** Add at least one exercise from a non-U.S. perspective. Restructure the Chapter 9 exercise. Trim Chapter 10 exercise from five to three domains. Consolidate to a single source for exercises.

## Part IV: Front Matter and Bibliography Assessment

### Preface (8.0/10)
Well-written and comprehensive. Clearly articulates the book's purpose, audience, structure, and analytical commitments. The "How to Use This Book" sections for instructors, students, and practitioners are practical.

### Glossary (7.5/10)
Thorough coverage of key terms, acronyms, and Chinese-language concepts. The Chinese Terms section is a distinguishing feature. Minor gap: some technical terms used in the text (e.g., "multi-patterning," "DUV lithography") are not in the glossary.

### Bibliography (6.5/10)
Includes important works across books, journal articles, government reports, and Chinese sources. However:
- Not organized consistently (some articles appear under "Books and Monographs")
- Baldwin's *Economic Statecraft* is listed twice (1985 and 2020 editions)
- Some entries are vague (e.g., "Brookings Institution. Various reports on U.S.-China economic relations")
- Does not serve as a master bibliography -- many in-text citations across chapters are absent

## Part V: Top 15 Recommendations (Prioritized)

### Must-Fix Before Publication

1. **Conduct a systematic fact-check** of all quantitative claims, correcting the errors identified above (WTO ruling date, Phase One shortfall inconsistency, GERD date, grain embargo date, Rand figures, EDA ownership, etc.)

2. **Perform a citation audit** across all chapters, ensuring every in-text citation has a corresponding reference entry, eliminating anachronistic citations, and standardizing format

3. **Integrate the missing figures** into their respective chapters and fix figure numbering discontinuities

4. **Create data codebooks** for all CSV files documenting sources, download dates, variable definitions, and whether values are actual or projected

5. **Resolve the Phase One purchase shortfall inconsistency** ($140B vs. $288B in Chapter 6) -- this is the most visible internal contradiction

### High Priority

6. **Reduce total manuscript length by 20-25%** through eliminating cross-chapter redundancy, consolidating Government Tools Boxes with main text, and tightening verbose sections

7. **Establish "home chapters" for major cases** (October 2022 controls → Ch. 4; rare earth dispute → Ch. 9; Huawei Mate 60 Pro → Ch. 4) and use brief cross-references elsewhere

8. **Run colorblind accessibility checks** on all figures and replace problematic color combinations

9. **Add at least one tabletop exercise from a non-U.S. perspective**

10. **Add formal case studies to Chapter 2** (rare earth dispute) and **a dedicated Iran/JCPOA case study to Chapter 7**

### Valuable Improvements

11. **Add a visual diagram of the four-dimensional framework** in Chapter 1 -- the framework is the book's intellectual backbone and deserves a clear visual representation

12. **Strengthen treatment of underrepresented topics:** services trade (Ch. 6), outbound investment screening (Ch. 8), sanctions evasion networks (Ch. 7), water security (Ch. 3), and domestic political economy (Ch. 1)

13. **Add package management (`renv`)** to the R visualization pipeline for reproducibility

14. **Update data to 2024-2025 baselines** throughout (lithium production, Treasury holdings, CIPS volumes, etc.)

15. **Add a brief European/allied perspective** to Chapter 10's policy recommendations to broaden the book's utility for international audiences

## Final Assessment

This textbook is tackling one of the most important topics in contemporary international relations, and it does so with genuine analytical sophistication, impressive breadth, and engaging prose. The consistent integration of Chinese perspectives, the inclusion of original Chinese-language terminology, and the commitment to empirical evidence distinguish it from existing treatments. The analytical framework is well-designed and the pedagogical features (discussion questions, tabletop exercises, hint boxes) reflect serious thought about how students learn.

The manuscript's weaknesses are primarily editorial rather than intellectual. The excessive length, citation problems, and factual errors are all fixable with disciplined editing and fact-checking. The data visualization concerns require attention but the underlying chart designs are solid. The U.S.-centrism of the exercises is a real limitation but can be addressed by adding 1-2 exercises from alternative perspectives.

With the recommended revisions, this could be the definitive graduate textbook on economic coercion for the 2025-2030 period -- a field that desperately needs one. The foundation is strong; the task now is refinement.

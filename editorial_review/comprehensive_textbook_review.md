# Comprehensive Textbook Review: *Economic Coercion and Strategic Competition*

**Reviewer:** Claude (AI-assisted editorial review)
**Date:** February 23, 2026
**Author:** Laurence Wilse-Samson, Columbia SIPA

---

## Overall Score: 7.5 / 10

**Summary verdict:** An ambitious, well-conceived, and engagingly written graduate-level textbook that fills a genuine gap in the literature on economic statecraft. The analytical frameworks are strong, the pedagogical materials (especially the tabletop exercises) are exceptional, and the sustained engagement with Chinese perspectives is a genuine differentiator. However, significant citation deficiencies, several factual errors, inconsistent chapter structure, and excessive length in places prevent this from reaching the standard required for academic publication without substantial revision.

---

## I. WHAT WORKS WELL

### 1. Fills a Real Gap in the Literature

The textbook addresses a genuine need. Existing treatments of economic statecraft tend to focus narrowly on sanctions (Hufbauer et al., Nephew), miss the technology dimension (most IR textbooks), or lack practitioner-oriented analysis. This book integrates trade controls, financial sanctions, technology competition, supply chain vulnerabilities, information infrastructure, and industrial policy into a single coherent framework. The scope is impressive and well-justified.

### 2. Exceptional Writing Quality

The prose is unusually strong for an academic textbook — clear, authoritative, and occasionally memorable without sacrificing analytical precision. Examples:

- "Washington bureaucrats did something rarely seen in the annals of trade policy: they attempted to surgically remove a rival superpower's brain" (Ch. 1)
- "Each country was punching itself in the face, insisting the other would soon cry uncle" (Ch. 6)
- "We may escape the petro-states only to find ourselves beholden to the battery-state" (Ch. 3)
- "The invisible hand has been replaced by the visible fist" (Ch. 8)
- "We are sawing off the branch we're sitting on" (Ch. 10)

The author maintains an opinionated but fair voice throughout — willing to challenge conventional wisdom while presenting evidence honestly.

### 3. Strong Analytical Frameworks

The four-dimensional framework (Domain, Target, Objective, Intensity) and five effectiveness criteria (Target Compliance, Capability Degradation, Cost Imposition, Sustainability, Collateral Damage) introduced in Chapter 1 are genuinely useful analytical tools. When applied consistently — as in the semiconductor export controls case study (Ch. 4) and the Australia case study (Ch. 8) — they give students a replicable method for evaluating economic coercion.

### 4. Sustained Chinese Perspectives

The "Chinese Perspective" boxes in every chapter are the textbook's most distinctive feature. They go well beyond token inclusion:

- Chinese-language terms with pinyin romanization and characters throughout (卡脖子, 双循环, 韬光养晦, 东升西降)
- Historical context rooted in the Century of Humiliation narrative
- Internal Chinese debates (nationalist hardliners vs. economic pragmatists)
- Chinese-language academic and government sources in the bibliography

This reflects genuine scholarly engagement rather than superficial "both-sides" gestures.

### 5. Outstanding Tabletop Exercises

The 10 tabletop exercises in Appendix A are the textbook's strongest pedagogical feature — arguably publication-ready as a standalone resource. Each exercise provides:

- Realistic, data-grounded scenarios
- 6 clearly defined participant roles reflecting real interagency dynamics
- Escalatory injects forcing increasingly difficult decisions
- Detailed facilitator notes with common pitfalls
- Background materials citing specific legal authorities

These would not be out of place at the National War College or State Department's Foreign Service Institute.

### 6. Impressive Currency

The textbook incorporates developments through late 2025 / early 2026, including: BIOSECURE Act enactment (December 2025), TikTok USDS Joint Venture (January 2026), BIS December 2025 export control updates, and current CIPS/SWIFT data. This level of currency is unusual and valuable.

### 7. Data Infrastructure

59 original R/ggplot2 visualizations, 28 CSV datasets, and a shared theme system represent serious investment in reproducible data visualization. The Data Sources sections at the end of each chapter are genuinely useful reference guides.

## II. WHAT NEEDS IMPROVEMENT

### 1. Citation Deficiency (CRITICAL)

**This is the textbook's most serious problem.** The project's own citation audit found only an **11% inline citation rate** across Chapters 2-5 — meaning ~89% of quantitative claims lack proper attribution. While some statistics are well-sourced (particularly in Chapters 6 and 7), the majority of specific figures throughout the book appear without citations.

Examples of high-profile uncited claims:
- "90-95% of antibiotics used in the United States depend on precursors from China" (Ch. 2)
- "China produces 80% of solar panels" (Ch. 3)
- "TSMC Arizona fabs face 30-50% cost disadvantages" (Ch. 2)
- "$10 trillion daily via submarine cables" (Ch. 5)
- "GDPR fines against Google exceeding 8 billion euros" (Ch. 5)
- Tariff averages on Chinese goods (Ch. 6)
- CIPS processing volumes (Ch. 7, Ch. 10)
- BRICS+ GDP share (Ch. 10)

For an academic textbook, this is a disqualifying deficiency. **Every quantitative claim needs a specific, verifiable citation** — not just an institutional name (e.g., "USGS 2023") but a specific report title or URL where the reader can verify the figure.

### 2. Factual Errors (Some Corrected, Some Remaining)

The citation remediation process caught several significant errors, but some remain:

**Corrected (per remediation report):**
- South African demographics: originally "Whites (13%)" — corrected to ~20%
- U.S. import decline from China: originally "21%" — corrected to 16.2%
- Soviet grain embargo tonnage: originally 34.8M tons — corrected to ~31M
- Reserve currency share: standardized to ~58% across chapters

**Still requiring attention:**
- **Ch. 2:** Intel CEO "resigned in 2020" — Pat Gelsinger replaced Bob Swan in February 2021, not 2020, and the characterization as "resignation" is debatable
- **Ch. 2:** ARM described as "potentially facing sale" — ARM IPO'd in September 2023; this is outdated
- **Ch. 3:** Ethiopia's Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam described as "completed in 2020" — construction was still ongoing as of 2024
- **Ch. 5:** "GDPR fines against Google exceeding 8 billion euros" conflates GDPR fines with separate antitrust penalties
- **Ch. 6:** Amiti et al. 2019 cited as "(AER)" — actually published in *Journal of Economic Perspectives*; Fajgelbaum et al. 2020 cited as "(JPE)" — actually published in *Quarterly Journal of Economics*
- **Ch. 7:** "U.S. humanitarian exports to Iran fell 90% (2017-2019)" attributed to Nephew 2017 — chronologically impossible since the book predates the period
- **Ch. 7:** CIPS volume inconsistency ($50-60B daily vs. $24T annual doesn't reconcile cleanly)
- **Ch. 8:** "CFIUS reviewed 770 transactions... only 5 were withdrawn or prohibited" — likely understates withdrawals
- **Ch. 8:** TikTok narrative inconsistency between Executive Summary ("remains Chinese-owned") and later section (describing January 2026 Joint Venture)
- **Ch. 10:** Lithium production figure (130,000 tons) appears outdated — USGS reports ~180,000 tons for 2023
- **Antibiotic dependency claim (90-95% from China)** remains unresolved — recent studies suggest 60-70%

### 3. Inconsistent Chapter Structure

The textbook promises a consistent template but does not deliver one uniformly:

| Feature | Ch.1 | Ch.2 | Ch.3 | Ch.4 | Ch.5 | Ch.6 | Ch.7 | Ch.8 | Ch.9 | Ch.10 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Executive Summary | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Case Studies | 2 | 0 | 0* | 1 | 2 | 2 | 0* | 2 | 2 | 0 |
| Chinese Perspective | ✗ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Gov't Tools Boxes | 2 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 2 | 2 | 0* | 2 | 0 | 0 |
| Discussion Questions | ✗ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Key Insights | ✗ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| European/Global South Box | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ | ✓ | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ |

*0 = case material woven into narrative but not in formal case study boxes

Chapter 1 is missing Discussion Questions and Key Insights. Several chapters lack formal case studies despite the book roadmap promising them. The European and Global South perspective boxes appear sporadically (only Ch. 6 and Ch. 7) rather than consistently.

**Recommendation:** Adopt a strict chapter template and ensure every chapter includes: Executive Summary, Chinese Perspective Box, at least one formal Case Study with framework application, Government Tools Box (where applicable), Key Insights, and Discussion Questions.

### 4. Excessive Length and Redundancy

Individual chapter word counts range from ~9,800 (Ch. 10) to ~19,500 (Ch. 2):

| Chapter | Word Count | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| Ch. 1 | ~15,900 | Long for an intro chapter |
| Ch. 2 | ~19,500 | Too long — significant overlap between overview and semiconductor sections |
| Ch. 3 | ~11,100 | Appropriate |
| Ch. 4 | ~17,000 | Too long — case study repeats semiconductor section material |
| Ch. 5 | ~15,700 | Broad; some sections survey-level |
| Ch. 6 | ~19,000 | Too long — trade war covered twice (main text + case study) |
| Ch. 7 | ~13,000 | Good |
| Ch. 8 | ~13,100 | Good |
| Ch. 9 | ~10,300 | Good |
| Ch. 10 | ~9,800 | Slightly thin on some topics |

**Total estimated: ~144,400 words** (main chapters only, excluding front/back matter and appendix)

The primary source of bloat is **redundancy between main text sections and formal case studies**. In Chapters 4 and 6 especially, the semiconductor export controls and U.S.-China trade war are analyzed in detail in the main text and then again in the case study, with substantially overlapping content. Either integrate the case study into the main narrative or differentiate them much more sharply.

A target of **12,000-14,000 words per chapter** would improve readability without sacrificing substance. Chapters 2, 4, and 6 each need ~3,000-5,000 words cut.

### 5. Missing and Misplaced Figures

Several issues with figure integration:

- **Missing from text:** Figures 9.2, 9.4, 9.5, 10.2, 10.4, 10.5 exist in the `/figures/` directory but are never referenced in chapter text
- **Numbering gaps:** Figure 1.3 is never referenced (numbering jumps from 1.2 to 1.4); Figure 6.2 likewise skipped
- **Cross-chapter sourcing:** Figure 5.1 sources from a Chapter 3 figure file (`fig_03_05_subsea_cables.png`) — should have its own figure or be explicitly cross-referenced
- **Misplaced:** Figure 8.1 (BRI infrastructure) appears in the Executive Summary but BRI is not the chapter's primary focus
- **Insufficient figures:** Chapter 5 has only 1 figure for ~15,700 words of quantitative content; Chapter 4 has only 2 figures for ~17,000 words

### 6. Uneven Depth Across Topics

Some topics receive outsized treatment while others are compressed:

- **Semiconductors** are covered extensively in Ch. 2, Ch. 4, and Ch. 6 — arguably too much overlap
- **Water security** is framed as the "ultimate existential resource" (Ch. 3) but receives only ~500 words
- **Quantum computing, biotechnology, and space** in Ch. 4 receive cursory treatment (~300-400 words each) compared to AI and semiconductors
- **SOEs and sovereign wealth funds** in Ch. 8 are underdeveloped (~1,500 words for a complex topic)
- **Historical cases** of Napoleon and Jefferson in Ch. 9 are too thin to be analytically useful (~400 words each) — either expand or cut
- **Scenario D** (Renewed Integration) in Ch. 10 reads as an afterthought

### 7. Bibliography Structural Issues

- Some books are listed under "Journal Articles and Reports" (e.g., Miller 2022, Mulder 2022, Nephew 2017)
- Several entries are vague aggregates ("Brookings Institution. Various reports... 2018-2024") rather than specific citations
- At least one questionable entry: "Liao, Rebecca, and David Dollar. *China's Financial System*. Brookings, 2020" could not be immediately confirmed
- Missing key works: quantitative sanctions effectiveness literature (Bapat and Morgan, Early), international economic law (Lester and Mercurio), and humanitarian impact scholarship (Joy Gordon)

### 8. Glossary Gaps

The glossary (117 entries) is a good foundation but is missing several terms central to the textbook's subject matter:

- Decoupling / de-risking
- Friend-shoring / near-shoring / reshoring
- Foreign Direct Product Rule (FDPR)
- Correspondent banking
- Belt and Road Initiative (BRI)
- CBDC / digital yuan
- Wassenaar Arrangement
- Military-civil fusion (军民融合)
- Compellence vs. deterrence vs. denial
- Overcompliance / de-risking (financial)

Minor error: ASML expanded as "Advanced Semiconductor Materials Lithography" — the company no longer uses this expansion.

## IV. PRIORITY RECOMMENDATIONS

### Tier 1: Must-Fix Before Publication

1. **Systematic citation pass:** Add inline citations to every quantitative claim. Target: 90%+ citation rate. This alone would likely require 2-3 weeks of dedicated research assistant time.

2. **Correct remaining factual errors:** Intel CEO timeline, ARM ownership, GERD completion, journal citation errors (AER→JEP, JPE→QJE), antibiotic dependency claim, CIPS volume reconciliation, Nephew 2017 chronological impossibility.

3. **Standardize chapter structure:** Add Discussion Questions and Key Insights to Chapter 1. Add a Chinese Perspective box to Chapter 1. Ensure every chapter has at least one formal case study applying the four-dimensional framework.

4. **Integrate missing figures:** Figures 9.4, 9.5, 10.2, 10.4, 10.5 exist but are unreferenced. Either integrate them or remove from the repository.

### Tier 2: Strongly Recommended

5. **Reduce chapter length:** Cut Chapters 2, 4, and 6 by 3,000-5,000 words each, primarily by eliminating redundancy between main text and case studies.

6. **Add figures to thin chapters:** Chapter 5 needs at least 2-3 additional figures (data localization map, standards patent shares, cyber incident timeline). Chapter 4 needs a semiconductor supply chain diagram and technology restriction timeline.

7. **Expand the glossary:** Add the ~10-15 missing terms listed above. Fix the ASML expansion.

8. **Clean up the bibliography:** Reclassify misplaced entries, replace vague aggregates with specific citations, verify the Liao/Dollar entry, and add missing works from the sanctions effectiveness and international economic law literatures.

9. **Reconcile data inconsistencies:** Standardize TSMC market share, CIPS volumes, reserve currency percentages, and rare earth processing shares across all chapters.

### Tier 3: Would Strengthen the Book

10. **Add European and Global South perspective boxes** to more chapters. Currently only Chapters 6 and 7 include non-Chinese alternative perspectives. Chapters 3 (resource nationalism), 8 (BRI), and 9 (historical cases) would benefit.

11. **Expand thin sections:** Water security (Ch. 3), SOEs/SWFs (Ch. 8), quantum/biotech/space (Ch. 4), historical comparisons (Ch. 9), and Scenario D (Ch. 10).

12. **Add a Chinese decision-maker exercise** to Appendix A — one where students play roles in the Chinese system rather than the U.S. interagency.

13. **Create data provenance documentation** for CSV files in `/data/sources/` — each file should have a header or companion document noting the original source, access date, and any transformations applied.

14. **Consider splitting Chapter 4** into two chapters: (a) Semiconductors and AI, (b) Emerging Technologies (quantum, bio, space). This would allow deeper treatment of currently underdeveloped topics.

## V. COMPARISON TO EXISTING TEXTBOOKS

| Feature | This Textbook | Blackwill & Harris (2016) | Nephew (2017) | Hufbauer et al. (2007) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Scope | Comprehensive | Broad but thinner | Sanctions only | Sanctions only |
| Technology coverage | Excellent | Minimal | None | None |
| Chinese perspectives | Extensive | Limited | None | None |
| Data/visualizations | 59 original figures | Few | Some | Extensive |
| Pedagogical materials | Exceptional (exercises) | None | Limited | None |
| Empirical rigor | Moderate (citation gaps) | Moderate | High | High |
| Currency | 2025-2026 | 2016 | 2017 | 2007 |

The textbook occupies a genuinely distinctive position: no existing single-volume work combines this breadth of coverage (trade, finance, technology, supply chains, information) with this level of pedagogical investment (exercises, frameworks, visualizations) and this depth of Chinese perspective integration. The closest competitor would be Drezner (2021) *The Uses and Abuses of Weaponized Interdependence*, which is an edited volume rather than a textbook, or Farrell and Newman (2023) *Underground Empire*, which is trade-press rather than pedagogical.

---

## VI. FINAL ASSESSMENT

This textbook has the potential to become the standard graduate-level text on economic statecraft and great power competition. The analytical frameworks are sound, the writing is engaging, the Chinese perspectives are genuine, and the tabletop exercises alone justify adoption.

However, it cannot be published in its current form. The citation deficiency is disqualifying for an academic press. The factual errors, while mostly caught in the audit, suggest the need for a thorough fact-checking pass by research assistants with access to primary sources. The structural inconsistencies and excessive length in some chapters require editorial discipline.

**With a dedicated revision addressing Tier 1 and Tier 2 recommendations, this would be an 8.5-9/10 textbook — genuinely the best available treatment of economic coercion for graduate education.**

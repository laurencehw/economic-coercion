## Section 2: Defining Key Concepts (2,000 words)

### 2.1 Geoeconomics (600 words)

**Etymology and Evolution**:
- Edward Luttwak's 1990 article: "From geopolitics to geo-economics"
- Original meaning: Using economic tools with logic of conflict
- Current usage: Intersection of economics, geography, and strategic competition

**Definition (build from literature)**:
> "Geoeconomics is the use of economic instruments to pursue or defend strategic objectives, or the geopolitical consequences of economic phenomena."

**Core characteristics**:
1. **Instrumental**: Economics as means to geopolitical ends (not economic efficiency)
2. **Strategic**: Anticipates adversary responses; multi-move thinking
3. **Competitive**: Zero-sum elements even in non-military competition
4. **State-centric**: Government direction, not just market forces

**Relationship to other concepts**:
- **Geopolitics**: Geography + power; geoeconomics adds economic dimension
- **International Political Economy (IPE)**: Academic field studying politics-economics interaction
- **Geo-economics vs. economics**: Profit vs. power; efficiency vs. security

**Scholarly debate**:
- Is geoeconomics analytically useful or just rebranding? (Wigell & Scholvin, Kim debates)
- Risk of overgeneralization - what ISN'T geoeconomics?

### 2.2 Economic Statecraft (500 words)

**Definition (David Baldwin)**:
> "Economic statecraft involves the use of economic means to pursue foreign policy goals."

**Baldwin's taxonomy** (Economic Statecraft, 1985):
- **Positive statecraft**: Incentives (aid, market access, investment)
- **Negative statecraft**: Punishment (sanctions, embargoes, restrictions)

**Spectrum of economic statecraft**:
```
Cooperation ←→ Coercion ←→ Conflict
    ↓              ↓             ↓
Trade deals   Sanctions      Blockade
FDI flows     Export controls Economic warfare
```

**Key distinction from economic policy**:
- **Economic policy**: Maximize national welfare, growth, employment
- **Economic statecraft**: Achieve political/security objectives (may sacrifice welfare)

**Examples across the spectrum**:
- **Cooperation**: Marshall Plan (rebuild Europe to prevent Soviet expansion)
- **Coercion**: Trump tariffs on China (force trade concessions)
- **Conflict**: WWII blockades (destroy enemy economy)

### 2.3 Weaponized Interdependence (500 words)

**Farrell & Newman framework** (International Security, 2019; Underground Empire, 2023):

**Core insight**:
- Globalization created network structures with chokepoints
- States controlling chokepoints can weaponize them
- Not just bilateral dependencies - network position matters

**Two mechanisms**:
1. **Panopticon effect**: Information dominance via network hubs (e.g., SWIFT messages give US visibility)
2. **Chokepoint effect**: Denial of access via network hubs (e.g., SWIFT exclusion isolates target)

**Key examples**:
- **Financial**: SWIFT payment system (Belgium-based but US influenced)
- **Internet**: DNS root servers; internet backbone infrastructure
- **Trade**: Container shipping; critical ports
- **Technology**: Semiconductor manufacturing equipment (ASML, TSMC)

**Asymmetries**:
- US benefits from dollar centrality, technology leadership, network control
- China vulnerable but building alternatives (CIPS, BRI, tech self-sufficiency)

**Implications**:
- Integration ≠ vulnerability; position in network determines leverage
- Weaponization accelerates de-globalization as states seek autonomy
- "Efficient" global networks vs. "resilient" redundant networks trade-off

### 2.4 Economic Coercion (400 words)

**Definition** (for this book):
> "Economic coercion is the use of economic instruments by states to compel, deter, or punish other states, achieving security objectives through economic pressure rather than military force."

**Characteristics**:
1. **Intentional**: Deliberate state policy, not market forces
2. **Coercive**: Designed to change target's behavior against their preferences
3. **Economic instruments**: Trade, finance, investment, technology, etc.
4. **Strategic objectives**: National security, foreign policy, geopolitical goals

**Scope of this book**:
- **Primary focus**: US-China strategic competition
- **Secondary focus**: Russia, EU, other major actors
- **Historical cases**: Cold War, earlier examples for lessons
- **Excluded**: Purely economic disputes (e.g., trade balances) without strategic dimension

**Relationship to economic warfare**:
- **Economic coercion**: Short of warfare; target can choose to comply or resist
- **Economic warfare**: Destructive; aim to cripple enemy economy (WWII blockades)
- **Gray zone**: Where does coercion end and warfare begin? (Ukraine 2022 sanctions debate)

## Government Tools Box 2: National Emergencies Act (NEA) (800 words)

### Legal Authority

**Statute**: National Emergencies Act (NEA) of 1976, 50 U.S.C. §§ 1601-1651

**Key Provisions**:
Provides the procedural framework for presidential declaration of national emergencies. Does not grant independent substantive powers, but activates emergency authorities under other statutes (like IEEPA). Requires:
- Presidential declaration identifying statutory authorities to be used
- Transmission to Congress and Federal Register publication
- Annual renewal
- Congressional termination option (joint resolution)
- Automatic termination if not renewed

**Limitations**:
- Must specify which emergency authorities are being invoked
- Subject to judicial review (though courts generally defer)
- Congress can terminate by joint resolution (though subject to presidential veto)
- Must renew annually, creating oversight opportunity
- Emergency must be genuine threat (though broadly interpreted)

### Implementing Agency

**Primary**: Executive Office of the President (President's authority)

**Supporting**:
- All federal agencies gain emergency powers under respective statutes
- Federal Register: Publication and tracking
- Congress: Oversight and potential termination

**Regulatory Framework**:
- Individual emergency declarations published in Federal Register
- Each emergency is separate and must be renewed annually
- Currently 40+ active national emergencies (many decades old)

### How It Works

**Trigger**: Presidential determination that emergency exists requiring extraordinary authorities

**Process**:
1. President identifies threat requiring emergency response
2. Issues proclamation declaring national emergency
3. Specifies which statutory emergency authorities are activated
4. Transmits declaration to Congress and publishes in Federal Register
5. Submits report within 30 days detailing authorities used
6. Reports every 6 months on emergency status
7. Must renew annually or emergency terminates

**Scope**:
- **Can do**: Activate emergency authorities in 136+ separate statutory provisions
- **Cannot do**: Create new authorities; must rely on existing statutes

**Duration**: Continues until terminated by President, Congress, or non-renewal (annual)

### Recent Applications (Selected)

**Example 1: Iran Emergency (1979-Present)**
- **Date**: November 1979 (oldest continuous emergency)
- **Target**: Iranian government
- **Rationale**: Hostage crisis; ongoing threat from Iran
- **Activated**: IEEPA authorities for sanctions
- **Status**: Renewed annually for 45+ years

**Example 2: COVID-19 Emergency (2020-2023)**
- **Date**: March 2020
- **Target**: Pandemic response (domestic)
- **Authorities**: Public health emergency authorities
- **Status**: Terminated May 2023

**Example 3: Ukraine Emergency (2014-Present)**
- **Date**: March 2014
- **Target**: Russia (response to Crimea annexation)
- **Activated**: IEEPA for Russia sanctions
- **Status**: Expanded February 2022; ongoing

### Strengths and Limitations

**Strengths**:
- **Speed**: President can act quickly without Congressional approval
- **Flexibility**: Broad range of authorities available
- **Durability**: Emergencies can continue for decades

**Limitations**:
- **Proliferation**: 40+ active emergencies dilute concept of "emergency"
- **Congressional oversight weak**: Termination requires joint resolution (veto risk)
- **Judicial deference**: Courts rarely overturn emergency declarations
- **Political**: Can become controversial (Trump border wall emergency)

### Further Reading

- CRS Report R46379: "The National Emergencies Act: Overview and Issues for Congress"
- Brennan Center: "A Guide to Emergency Powers and Their Use"
- Goitein & Shapiro: "Declared National Emergencies Under the National Emergencies Act"

## Perspective Box: Chinese View on Economic Statecraft and US Coercion (2,000 words)

### Historical Context and Formative Experiences

**Century of Humiliation (百年国耻) and Economic Exploitation (400 words)**

The period from 1839 (First Opium War) to 1949 fundamentally shapes how China views Western economic statecraft:

**Opium Wars (1839-1842, 1856-1860)**:
- British use of economic-military coercion to force open Chinese markets
- Unequal Treaties: Extraterritoriality, tariff autonomy loss, indemnities
- Lesson: Western powers will use economic leverage to subordinate China

**Semi-Colonial Period**:
- Foreign concessions in Shanghai, Tianjin, etc.
- Economic spheres of influence (British, French, German, Russian, Japanese)
- Customs Service controlled by foreigners
- Sovereign debt as tool of control
- Lesson: Economic integration under asymmetric power = exploitation

**Japanese Invasion**:
- Economic blockade and resource extraction
- "Co-Prosperity Sphere" as cover for domination
- Lesson: Economic interdependence without sovereignty is dangerous

**Modern Resonance**:
- Xi Jinping frequently references "national humiliation" (2012 onward)
- US semiconductor controls compared to 19th century technology denial
- Deep cultural memory: "Never again" will China be economically vulnerable

**Cold War Isolation (1949-1979)** (200 words)

**Western Embargo (CoCom)**:
- US and allies denied China technology, capital, market access
- Forced self-reliance (自力更生): "Two bombs, one satellite" achieved despite embargo
- But: China fell far behind economically during isolation

**Soviet Split (1960)**:
- USSR withdrawal of advisors and aid
- China survived but at great cost
- Lesson: Cannot rely on any great power; must have autonomous capabilities

**Contrasting lesson**: Self-reliance possible but costly; integration beneficial IF China has power to resist coercion

**Reform Era Double-Bind (1979-Present)** (200 words)

**Benefits of Integration**:
- WTO accession (2001): Massive economic growth
- Technology transfer via FDI, JVs, study abroad
- Rapid rise to second-largest economy

**Vulnerabilities Created**:
- Dependence on US semiconductors, software, financial system
- US market access critical for exports
- Dollar system gives US leverage
- Technology chokepoints (OS, chip design, manufacturing equipment)

**Current Chinese View**:
- Integration was beneficial but created dependencies
- US now weaponizing those dependencies
- Must achieve self-sufficiency in critical technologies
- "Dual circulation" strategy: Reduce external dependencies

**Sovereignty and Non-Interference**:

### Key Chinese Strategic Concepts

**综合国力** (zōnghé guólì) - "Comprehensive National Power" (CNP)

- **Definition**: Holistic measure of state power aggregating economic, military, technological, diplomatic, cultural, and governance dimensions
- **Origin**: Developed by Chinese strategists (Huang Shuofeng, Hu Angang) in 1980s-90s, drawing on Western concepts but with Chinese characteristics
- **Application**:
  - Economic coercion capabilities are component of CNP
  - China sees improving CNP as essential to resist Western pressure
  - Justifies massive state investment in technology, infrastructure, military
- **Current usage**: Xi emphasizes "comprehensive national security" (综合国家安全观) as extension of CNP concept

**双循环** (shuāng xúnhuán) - "Dual Circulation"

- **Definition**: Economic strategy with domestic circulation (内循环) as primary and international circulation (外循环) as supplement
- **Origin**: Xi Jinping articulated in May 2020 in response to COVID, trade war, and tech restrictions
- **Components**:
  - **Domestic circulation**: Expand domestic consumption, reduce export dependence, indigenous innovation
  - **International circulation**: Maintain engagement where beneficial, but on China's terms
- **Application to economic coercion**:
  - Reduce vulnerabilities from US sanctions/restrictions
  - Build domestic alternatives to foreign technologies
  - Use BRI and RCEP to create China-centered trade networks
  - Not full decoupling, but strategic hedging

**不可靠实体清单** (bùkěkào shítǐ qīngdān) - "Unreliable Entity List"

- **Definition**: Chinese government list of foreign entities that violate market rules or harm Chinese interests
- **Origin**: Announced 2019 in response to US Entity List designations of Huawei
- **Purpose**:
  - Deterrence: Warn foreign companies against cooperating with US sanctions
  - Retaliation: Punish companies that "discriminate" against Chinese firms
  - Ambiguity: Vague criteria create self-censorship
- **Reality**: More threat than action; few entities actually designated
- **Significance**: Shows Chinese willingness to weaponize market access

## Section 4: Conceptual Framework for Analysis (1,500 words)

[This section provides the analytical framework used throughout the book]

### 4.1 Dimensions of Economic Coercion (600 words)

**Four key dimensions to analyze any case of economic coercion**:

**1. Domain**: What economic realm is being targeted?
- Trade (tariffs, quotas, embargoes)
- Finance (sanctions, asset freezes, payment system exclusion)
- Technology (export controls, investment restrictions)
- Energy/Resources (supply cutoffs, embargoes)
- Investment (FDI controls, divestment)
- Multiple domains (comprehensive approach)

**2. Target**: Who/what is being pressured?
- State-level: Government behavior
- Sector-level: Specific industries
- Entity-level: Companies, individuals
- Alliance-level: Pressuring bloc of countries

**3. Objective**: What is coercer trying to achieve?
- **Compellence**: Force target to do something (change policy, release prisoners)
- **Deterrence**: Prevent target from doing something (invade, proliferate)
- **Punishment**: Impose costs without specific behavioral goal (express displeasure)
- **Signaling**: Demonstrate resolve, commitment, or capability
- **Weakening**: Degrade adversary's long-term power (attrition)

**4. Intensity**: How severe is the coercion?
- Symbolic (mild tariffs, targeted sanctions)
- Moderate (sector-specific restrictions, secondary sanctions)
- Severe (comprehensive embargo, financial system exclusion)
- Extreme (blockade, economic warfare)

### 4.2 Effectiveness Criteria (500 words)

**How to evaluate if economic coercion "works"?**

**Challenge**: Depends on objectives, which may be multiple or unstated

**Five criteria**:

1. **Goal Achievement**: Did target comply with demands?
   - Problem: Demands may be vague or multiple
   - Problem: Compliance may be partial or delayed
   - Problem: Other factors (military threats, domestic politics) may also matter

2. **Cost Imposition**: Did coercion inflict economic pain on target?
   - Measurable: GDP effects, market disruptions, capital flight
   - But: Pain ≠ policy change; regimes can tolerate costs

3. **Sending Signals**: Did coercion convey resolve or commitment?
   - Relevant for deterrence or alliance reassurance
   - Hard to measure; depends on audience perception

4. **Constraining Capabilities**: Did coercion weaken adversary long-term?
   - Example: Export controls slow China's military modernization
   - Time horizon matters: Short-term pain vs. long-term adaptation

5. **Costs to Coercer**: What were unintended consequences or blowback?
   - Economic costs to own country
   - Diplomatic costs (allied friction)
   - Strategic costs (acceleration of adversary's countermeasures)

**Scholarly findings** (Hufbauer et al., Drezner):
- Economic sanctions succeed in only 20-40% of cases
- More likely to work when:
  - Target is smaller and economically dependent on coercer
  - Multilateral (coordinated) rather than unilateral
  - Goals are modest (release prisoners) not ambitious (regime change)
  - Target faces domestic pressure
- Less likely when:
  - Target's core security interests at stake
  - Target has alternative partners
  - Long duration allows adaptation

### 4.3 Strategic Considerations (400 words)

**Questions to ask about any economic coercion case**:

1. **Escalation dynamics**: How will target respond? Risk of spiral?
2. **Allied coordination**: Can coercer maintain coalition? Will defections occur?
3. **Second-order effects**: What are consequences beyond intended targets?
4. **Time horizons**: Short-term costs vs. long-term strategic effects?
5. **Off-ramps**: Is there face-saving way for target to comply?
6. **Credibility**: Will coercer follow through on threats? Enforce measures?
7. **Adaptation**: Will target develop workarounds (import substitution, alternative partners)?

**Trade-offs**:
- **Effectiveness vs. costs**: Stronger measures impose more costs on coercer too
- **Speed vs. sustainability**: Quick impacts vs. long-term viability
- **Breadth vs. depth**: Comprehensive sanctions vs. targeted "smart sanctions"
- **Unilateral vs. multilateral**: Speed/control vs. legitimacy/effectiveness

## Case Study 1: CoCom and Technology Denial to the Soviet Union (1,200 words)

**Purpose**: Historical precedent for technology export controls; longest-running case of coordinated Western economic coercion

[Full case study following template format]

### Background
- Coordinating Committee for Multilateral Export Controls (1949-1994)
- US + Western allies coordinated embargo on strategic goods to Soviet bloc
- Three-tier list: Munitions, atomic energy, industrial/commercial
- Administered through national export control systems

### The Coercive Action
- **Coercing States**: US, Western Europe, Japan
- **Target**: Soviet Union and Warsaw Pact
- **Objective**: Slow Soviet military-technological advancement; prevent West from contributing to own threat
- **Tools**: Export controls, licensing requirements, allied coordination
- **Timeline**: 40+ years (Cold War duration)

### Target Response
- Soviet indigenous development: Achieved nuclear weapons, space program despite embargo
- Industrial espionage: KGB technology acquisition efforts
- Alternative suppliers: Non-CoCom countries when possible
- Acceptance of inefficiency: Autarky in critical technologies

### Outcomes and Effectiveness
- **Short-term**: Slowed but did not prevent Soviet military modernization
- **Long-term**: Soviet economy fell behind West; competitiveness gap
- **Contribution to Cold War end**: Debated - was it decisive or marginal?
- **Empirical studies**: Soviet economy 2-3 decades behind West by 1980s

### Lessons Learned
1. **Allied coordination essential**: Unilateral controls easily evaded
2. **Time horizons matter**: Decades-long effort required for strategic effect
3. **Cannot prevent indigenous innovation**: Target will eventually develop capabilities
4. **Economic inefficiency accumulates**: Controls impose opportunity costs
5. **Enforcement challenges**: Smuggling, third-country diversion constant problems

### Relevance to US-China Competition
- **Parallels**: US seeking allied coordination on semiconductor controls (Netherlands, Japan)
- **Differences**:
  - China more integrated into global economy than USSR
  - China has far larger economy and technological base
  - Time horizon may be shorter (Chinese advancement faster)
  - Commercial vs. military distinction harder with dual-use tech
